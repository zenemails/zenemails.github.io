Wheel of Fortune Win-Back Campaign: Finding Signal in a List Nobody Was Watching

A dormant subscriber segment, surfaced during a data migration, was reactivated through a waterfall win-back sequence for Wheel of Fortune, a Sony Pictures Television production.

  • Client: Sony Pictures Television, Wheel of Fortune
  • Role: CRM Implementation, Message Testing & Performance Analysis (strategy shared with manager and data scientist)
  • Timeline: Three-wave campaign, Apr–Jun 2017, reported against a 12M-subscriber database
  • Tools: Salesforce Marketing Cloud (segmentation deployment, email build, send), SQL (audience segmentation, built by data scientist), Excel (performance reporting)
  • Scope: Win-back campaign execution, non-opener waterfall targeting, subject-line testing, performance reporting

Snapshot Overview

While supporting a Sony Pictures Television data migration and training the Wheel of Fortune team on Salesforce Marketing Cloud, a large segment of the subscriber database surfaced as never having been meaningfully engaged, not decayed, simply never activated. My manager greenlit a win-back experiment rather than suppressing the segment outright.

The resulting campaign used a three-wave waterfall structure, each wave narrowing to only the non-openers of the one before it, paired with subject-line testing in the final wave. I owned the tactical build, message testing, deployment, and performance reporting; a data scientist built the underlying SQL segmentation; my manager set the overall strategic direction. The system reactivated 880,000 subscribers and, based on post-campaign analysis, showed click-to-open rate improving sharply as the audience narrowed, evidence that later reused as a template for subsequent win-back campaigns.

The Problem

System problem: A meaningful segment of a 2B+ record, 17-business-unit shared subscriber database had never been folded into an active journey. There was no visibility into why, the list wasn't decayed through fatigue, it had simply never been activated, and no prior engagement data existed to inform a reactivation strategy.

User experience problem: Subscribers had no established relationship with the brand's email program. A single-message approach risked treating a large, undifferentiated group identically, when the underlying question, who among them was still reachable, could only be answered by observing behavior in real time.

Business constraint: This was framed internally as an experiment layered onto other concurrent workstreams (data migration, platform training, ISG reporting), not a dedicated initiative with its own research phase. Ownership was distributed across three people, which meant no single person controlled the full arc from diagnosis to strategy to execution.

My Role

Owned:

  • Loading segmented lists into Salesforce Marketing Cloud and deploying each wave
  • Building the email templates for each send
  • Proposing subject-line variants for team testing, including both lines tested in the final wave
  • Building and distributing wave-by-wave and campaign-by-campaign performance reports
  • Training the Wheel of Fortune team on Salesforce Marketing Cloud, the work that surfaced the dormant segment in the first place

Stakeholder involvement: My manager set the strategic direction and made the call to run a win-back experiment rather than suppress the segment. A data scientist built the SQL logic behind the audience segmentation and the non-opener waterfall targeting between waves. This is represented here as a three-person, shared-ownership effort, not an individually-led strategy engagement.

Designed and executed: My contribution sat on the tactical and analytical side, execution, message testing, and the reporting that surfaced the campaign's key insight, rather than the underlying segmentation architecture or the decision to run the experiment.

Process

1. Audit & System Diagnosis

  • Goal: Understand what was in the subscriber database during an unrelated migration and training effort.
  • Analyzed: Business unit data as part of migration cleanup, subscriber engagement history while training the Wheel of Fortune (WOF) team on the platform.
  • Decision: Escalate the dormant segment rather than let it pass as a byproduct of other work.
  • Why it mattered: The problem was found, not assigned, evidence of watching the data closely during unrelated tasks.

2. Insight Synthesis

  • Goal: Determine whether the dormant segment was worth reactivating or should be suppressed.
  • Analyzed: Segment size and inactivity against the shared 2B+ record database.
  • Decision: My manager chose to run a win-back experiment instead of a straight suppression.
  • Why it mattered: Kept a large segment recoverable rather than writing it off with no attempt to reactivate.

3. Journey & Segmentation Design

  • Goal: Reach the dormant segment without repeating the same blind send three times.
  • Designed: A three-wave, non-opener waterfall, each wave targeting only subscribers who hadn't opened the previous send.
  • Decision: Built by the data scientist and executed by me; narrowing the audience with intent rather than resending to the same group.
  • Why it mattered: Meant later waves reached a progressively more specific group, which is what ultimately produced the campaign's clearest signal.

4. Messaging / Narrative Architecture

  • Goal: Find the message that would move the narrowest, most resistant remaining segment.
  • Designed: Naming shifted from "Reminder" (Wave 1) to "Win_Back" (Wave 2–3); the final wave tested two subject-line directions, a loss-framed "Don't Lose a Turn!" against an urgency-framed "We're Taking You Off the List."
  • Decision: I proposed both variants; the team ran the test and selected the winner within three hours.
  • Why it mattered: The urgency framing won and scaled, and its performance lines up directly with the campaign's strongest engagement quality.

5. Implementation

  • Goal: Deploy each wave accurately against a live, shared 2B+ record database.
  • Executed: Loaded each segmented list into SFMC, built and deployed the emails for all three waves.
  • Decision: Tactical execution owned personally, segmentation logic and strategic direction owned by the data scientist and manager respectively.
  • Why it mattered: Kept the handoff between strategy and delivery tight across a three-person team.

6. Measurement & Iteration

  • Goal: Understand what actually happened once the campaign closed.
  • Analyzed: Re-engagement, re-attribution, CTR, open rate, CTOR, and delivery rate by wave and by individual campaign, emailed to both my department and the Wheel of Fortune team.
  • Decision: Treat the report as analysis, not just documentation, flag what stood out rather than only listing numbers.
  • Why it mattered: Surfaced the CTOR pattern that made this campaign a reusable reference point for later win-back efforts, even though the finding came after the experiment had already closed.

Key UX Insights

  • A narrowing, non-opener waterfall can improve engagement quality even as raw volume declines, fewer people responding isn't automatically a worse outcome if the remaining group is more genuinely reachable.
  • Urgency framing ("We're Taking You Off the List") outperformed loss-of-opportunity framing ("Don't Lose a Turn!") in the final, most-resistant wave, a signal about what actually moves a truly cold segment versus a mildly lapsed one.
  • The most valuable insight in this campaign was found retrospectively, in the post-campaign report, not in real time. Discovery that happens after a campaign closes still has value, but only if the reporting habit exists to catch it.
  • A dormant segment discovered as a byproduct of unrelated work (migration, training) is a common but easy-to-miss CRM problem; the habit of watching data closely during other tasks is what surfaced it here.
  • Distributed ownership across strategy, segmentation, and execution can still produce a coherent system, but only if reporting closes the loop back to everyone involved.
  • Without knowing why a list went cold, a reactivation sequence is designed blind; the biggest gap in this project was skipping that research question entirely.

Solution

The final system was a three-wave, non-opener waterfall reactivation sequence layered with subject-line testing in the final wave. Each wave excluded anyone who had opened a previous send, so the audience narrowed with intent rather than by attrition alone, later waves weren't the same message repeated, they were reaching a progressively more specific, more resistant group.

Naming conventions moved from "Reminder" in Wave 1 to "Win_Back" in Wave 2 and 3, tracking an escalation in message strategy. The final wave's subject-line test, urgency versus loss-aversion, was decided within three hours and the winner scaled to the remaining sends.

Performance reporting functioned as the system's feedback loop. It wasn't just documentation, it was the mechanism through which the CTOR pattern got identified and eventually reused as the starting template for later win-back campaigns.

Impact

Behavioral impact

  • 880,000 re-attributions across the 3-month, 12M-subscriber campaign
  • Click-to-open rate improved 59.7%, from 31.81% (Wave 1) to 50.79% (Wave 3)
  • Re-attribution volume declined 63.6% over the same span (519,754 → 189,337), the volume-vs-quality tradeoff at the center of this campaign's insight
  • Delivery rate held at 92–93% throughout

Business impact

  • The waterfall and subject-line-testing framework was reused as the template for subsequent win-back campaigns at Sony
  • Direct causation between the waterfall structure specifically and the CTOR improvement isn't established by this data alone, the clearest, most defensible link is between the urgency-framed subject line win and the strongest engagement quality of the campaign

Reflection

What worked: Proposing the subject-line test that ended up driving the strongest result, and reading the post-campaign report closely enough to catch the CTOR pattern instead of treating it as a formality.

What I'd improve: Understanding why the list had gone cold before designing the reactivation sequence, where it originated, what expectations subscribers had, if any, when they joined. We skipped that research question entirely and designed the win-back blind. I'd also want to close the insight loop faster; the CTOR pattern was found after the campaign had already ended, when it could have shaped how we approached later waves in the same campaign, not just future ones.

What this taught me: A good reporting habit, actually reading the data rather than just producing it, can surface strategic insight even inside a narrowly tactical role. It also taught me that shared ownership across strategy, segmentation, and execution can still produce something reusable, as long as someone closes the loop.
